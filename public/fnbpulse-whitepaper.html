Executive Summary

The gap isn't features. It's who the features are for.

Restaurant back-office software has never been short on tools. What's missing is one built for the segment that has the most operators in it and the least software attention: independent groups running two to twenty branches, who have outgrown a spreadsheet but will never be Restaurant365's or Toast Enterprise's ideal customer.

fnbPulse is a single, multi-tenant operating system for that group — sales, inventory, recipe and sub-recipe costing, supplier invoicing, procurement, and labor, all reading from one costing engine and one stock ledger, with an AI layer that does the data entry and the daily reading of the numbers that a busy owner-operator doesn't have time for.

The Market Today

Four ways operators currently solve this — and where each breaks

Talk to a restaurant group running more than one location and the back office almost always looks like one of these, or some combination:

  • Spreadsheets and a shoebox of invoices. Free, flexible, and the default for most independents — until recipe costs live in one file, inventory counts in another, and nobody trusts either after the third branch opens.
  • The POS vendor's bolt-on inventory module (Toast, Square, and similar). Ties you to that POS's hardware and pricing, and the costing logic is typically flat ingredient math with no real handling of sub-recipes, batch prep, or supplier-level procurement signal.
  • Standalone invoice/AP-automation tools. Solve OCR and bill-paying in isolation, but the extracted data doesn't know what a "dish" or a "recipe" is — it stops at the invoice, leaving costing as someone's separate manual job.
  • Enterprise back-office ERPs (Restaurant365, Apicbase, and similar). Genuinely comprehensive — and built, priced, and implemented for chains with dedicated IT and controllers, with onboarding measured in months, not an afternoon.

The pattern across all four: the moment a group has more than one location, they either pay enterprise money for enterprise complexity, or keep stitching point tools together and eating the reconciliation cost themselves.

What We Built Differently

Eight decisions that show up in the product, not just the pitch

01

Sub-recipes cost correctly, not approximately

Most costing tools flatten every recipe to raw ingredients. fnbPulse's Prep Items let a kitchen cost a house sauce, dough, or stock once — from its own ingredient lines and a batch yield — and then use it in any dish exactly like a raw ingredient, including its own recipe-unit conversion (track yield in kilograms, enter recipe quantities in grams) so kitchen staff enter numbers in the units they actually think in.

Cost changes cascade automatically: reprice one raw ingredient and every Prep Item built from it, and every dish built from that Prep Item, recosts without anyone touching a second screen.

02

OCR that degrades gracefully instead of failing hard

Invoice scanning runs a chained fallback — Document AI, then Gemini vision, then a Hugging Face vision model — advancing only when a provider is genuinely unavailable, never silently retrying on a bad read. A local model (Ollama) is available for operators who want it, but deliberately excluded from the automatic chain so a slow local inference never masquerades as a hang.

One vendor going down doesn't take invoice processing down with it — a real resilience property most single-provider OCR tools don't have.

03

An AI Operations Advisor that reads the numbers for you

Instead of another dashboard to interpret, the system generates a daily, plain-language briefing — 3 to 5 findings, each tagged Critical, Watch, or Opportunity, each with a dollar-impact estimate — cached once a day so it's a habit to check, not a bill to run.

Built on the same underlying signals (Pulse Score, Menu Matrix, Supplier Spend) already computed server-side — the Advisor narrates data the system already trusts, rather than a separate black box.

04

A cost simulator, before the price hike hits

Model a hypothetical ingredient price change and immediately see which active dishes are affected and which newly cross the food-cost target — before a supplier's increase actually lands on an invoice. The same costing math used everywhere else runs against hypothetical prices, including full cascade through Prep Items.

05

Supplier scorecards, not just a price list

Beyond "who's cheapest," the system tracks delivery cadence (is this supplier's next delivery overdue against their own history?) and price volatility (is this ingredient's price from this supplier swinging more than it should) — the two signals that actually predict a supplier relationship going bad before the invoice does.

06

Purchase orders draft themselves

When an ingredient drops to its reorder level, a draft purchase order sizes itself against the branch's par level, groups by each ingredient's cheapest known supplier, and waits for a human to review and send — closing the gap between "low stock warning" and "order placed" without removing the person from the loop.

07

Onboarding measured in minutes, not weeks

Photograph a paper menu and the system drafts dish names, prices, and likely recipes into an editable template — the single biggest barrier to adopting any costing system (typing in every dish and recipe by hand) collapsed into a review-and-confirm step.

08

Multi-branch from the first line of schema, not bolted on later

Every tenant-owned table carries its tenant scope natively; branches roll up to one company-level view or split out individually with the same selector used across every screen. Adding a fourth, fifth, or tenth branch is a form, not a migration.

Engineering Foundation

The parts that don't show up on a screenshot

The differentiators above are only real if the plumbing underneath them holds up under actual restaurant operations — multiple branches, live inventory, and money moving through invoices every day. Three decisions carry that weight:

  • One costing engine, everywhere. Dish cost, Prep Item cost, and the cost simulator all resolve through a single server-side module — not three copies of the same formula quietly drifting apart, which is exactly how homegrown spreadsheet systems lose everyone's trust.
  • An immutable stock ledger. Inventory is never overwritten in place; every sale, purchase, and correction is an append-only ledger row, with current stock as a derived, always-reconstructable view — so "why is this number wrong" always has an answer.
  • Graceful degradation as a house rule, not an exception. Every AI-dependent feature — OCR, the Advisor — falls back through alternative providers and degrades to a manual path rather than blocking the operator's day when one dependency has a bad afternoon.

Where This Goes Next

Built, in progress, and deliberately not yet started

In the interest of an honest whitepaper rather than a roadmap slide: usage-based billing, subdomain-per-tenant routing, and automated CI test runs are explicitly not built yet — tracked openly rather than glossed over. What's shipped and in daily use today is everything described above: multi-tenant sales, inventory, recipe/sub-recipe costing, invoicing with AI OCR, procurement, supplier intelligence, and the AI Advisor, running across Postgres with a fully migration-managed schema.

The thesis isn't "more features than the incumbents." It's that a 3-branch group and a 15-branch group both deserve a system that costs a batch of sauce correctly, reads their own numbers back to them every morning, and doesn't take a consultant to set up.
